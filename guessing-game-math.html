Solution

Let \(\alpha\) be the penalty weight, and \(p > q\).

Guessing stage

Player 1 and Player 2 observe independent signals

When players' information is independent, following one's signal is always optimal.

Player 2 observes Player 1's signal

Assuming that Player 1 follows their signal, Player 2 can either follow Player 1's signal and face the lottery \( (p\cdot 1,(1-p)\cdot (-\alpha)) \), or force a draw by choosing the opposite action.

Following P1's signal is optimal for P2 when \( p\ge\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha} \).

Signalling stage

When \( p\le\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha} \), Player 1's expected payoff from revealing the signal is 0 because Player 1 will force a draw. Player 1's expected payoff from sending an independent signal to Player 2 is \( pq-\alpha(1-p)(1-q) \). Therefore, it is optimal to send an independent signal if \(p\ge\frac{\alpha(1-q)}{q+\alpha(1-q)}\).

When \( p\ge\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha} \), Player 2 will follow Player 1's signal. Therefore, Player 1's payoff from showing her own signal is \( p-\alpha(1-p) \) and the payoff from sending an independent signal is \( pq-\alpha(1-p)(1-q) \), yielding the threshold \( p\ge\frac{\alpha q}{1-q + \alpha q} \).

Equilibria Summary

  • \( q \le p \le \frac{\alpha(1-q)}{q+\alpha(1-q)}\): P1 reveals her signal, P2 forces a draw, payoff 0. (This interval might be null. Another equivalent equilibrium is P1 reveals her signal, P2 follows P1's signal, P1 forces a draw by not following her signal.)
  • \( \frac{\alpha(1-q)}{q+\alpha(1-q)} \le p \le \frac{\alpha q}{1-q + \alpha q} \): P1 sends an independent signal, P2 follows the signal, payoff \(pq-\alpha(1-p)(1-q)\).
  • \( p \ge\frac{\alpha q}{1-q + \alpha q} \): P1 reveals her signal, P2 follows P1's signal, payoff \( p-\alpha(1-p) \).
